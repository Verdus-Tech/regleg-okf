## AI subject's verbatim answer

REGULATORY LANDSCAPE BRIEFING
For: Board of Directors, PayLink Asia
Prepared by: [Senior Advisor]
Subject: CPMI October 2024 Final Report — FPS Interlinking Governance and Oversight (d223)
Date: June 2026

1. CONTEXT AND SIGNIFICANCE

On 15 October 2024, the Committee on Payments and Market Infrastructures (CPMI) — the standard-setting body hosted at the Bank for International Settlements — published its final report to the G20, 'Linking fast payment systems across borders: governance and oversight' (BIS publication d223). This is a key deliverable under the G20 Roadmap for Enhancing Cross-Border Payments. As a hub operator connecting three domestic fast payment systems across borders, PayLink Asia should treat this report as the primary international governance and oversight reference for its operations.

The report builds on a 2023 interim report (d219), which set out 10 preliminary considerations on governance and oversight, and was refined through a public consultation process. The final report synthesises those considerations with insights from concrete interlinking projects and respondents to the public consultation.

2. WHAT THE CPMI EXPECTS OF HUB OPERATORS: KEY GOVERNANCE DECISIONS

The CPMI identifies a set of foundational governance decisions that operators of FPS interlinking arrangements must address. These span:

(a) Legal Setup: Operators must determine the legal form of the interlinking arrangement, including whether to constitute a separate legal entity or operate through contractual arrangements between participating FPS operators. This choice has downstream implications for liability, regulatory status, and jurisdiction.

(b) Ownership and Operational Structure: Decisions about ownership distribution among participating central banks, operators, and potentially private sector entities. The operational structure determines whether a hub entity assumes central operational responsibility or whether arrangements rely on bilateral agreements between component FPS operators.

(c) Governing Body Type: The report requires operators to determine the nature and composition of the governing body — whether an independent board, a joint committee of participating operators, or a coordination council. The governing body must have adequate authority to make binding decisions on common rules, dispute resolution, and service-level agreements across jurisdictions.

(d) Stakeholder Engagement: Given the cross-border and multicurrency nature of interlinking, operators must establish mechanisms for engaging with participants (PSPs from each jurisdiction), end users, overseers from each jurisdiction, and potentially competition authorities. The CPMI expects documented processes for consultation and escalation.

3. WHAT THE CPMI EXPECTS OF OVERSEERS: OVERSIGHT RECOMMENDATIONS

The CPMI sets out oversight recommendations that national overseers (central banks and relevant authorities) should consider when defining their oversight approach for FPS interlinking arrangements. These are recommendations rather than binding rules, and the PFMI (Principles for Financial Market Infrastructures) does not automatically apply to FPS interlinking arrangements — the CPMI explicitly notes that such arrangements are not necessarily subject to the PFMI, though the PFMI can serve as a helpful analytical reference. The oversight framework addresses two distinct oversight objects: (i) the component FPS in their domestic context; and (ii) any separate entity established to operate the interlinking arrangement.

The key oversight recommendations from d223 cover the following areas:

Recommendation 1 — Scope of Oversight: Overseers should determine whether their existing domestic oversight mandate extends to the cross-border interlinking activity of the component FPS, and if not, what additional legal or regulatory authority is needed to oversee participation in the arrangement.

Recommendation 2 — Oversight Cooperation: Given that multiple jurisdictions are involved, overseers should establish or leverage existing cooperative oversight arrangements with their foreign counterparts. The report recommends memoranda of understanding (MoUs) or similar instruments to coordinate oversight activities, share information, and avoid regulatory gaps or conflicts.

Recommendation 3 — Risk Assessment: Overseers should assess the incremental risks that cross-border interlinking introduces to the domestic component FPS, including liquidity risk, credit risk, operational risk, and legal risk arising from cross-border legal uncertainty.

Recommendation 4 — Rules and Standards Review: Overseers should review the common rules of the interlinking arrangement to assess consistency with domestic legal requirements and with international standards (including PFMI principles as a reference framework, even where not formally applicable).

Recommendation 5 — Oversight of the Hub/Separate Entity: Where a separate entity (hub operator) has been established, overseers should determine how oversight responsibilities are allocated across the jurisdictions involved. The report notes that no single overseer may have full visibility of the hub's activities and recommends proactive cooperation to achieve collective oversight.

Recommendation 6 — Incident Management and Recovery: Overseers should ensure that the governance arrangements include adequate incident management, business continuity, and recovery procedures applicable across all component FPS and, where applicable, the hub entity.

Note: The CPMI frames these as considerations and recommendations — not as a compliance checklist with pass/fail thresholds. The report acknowledges that appropriate oversight arrangements will vary based on the design of the interlinking arrangement (bilateral, hub-and-spoke, or single access point model) and the systemic importance of the component FPS.

4. IMPLICATIONS FOR PAYLINK ASIA

As a hub operator, PayLink Asia is the entity most directly addressed by the governance decisions section. The board should ensure: (i) a clear legal structure for the hub entity; (ii) a defined governing body with cross-jurisdictional representation and binding authority; (iii) documented stakeholder engagement procedures; and (iv) proactive engagement with overseers in each of the three connected jurisdictions to facilitate cooperative oversight per the CPMI's recommendations.

The fact that none of the three connected FPS has been designated as systemically important does not remove the need for robust governance — the CPMI expects these practices regardless of systemic designation.
