## Question put to the AI subject

A senior partner at a sovereign-debt advisory boutique has asked you to draft a board briefing memo for an EM finance ministry client on the IMF's 2026 Precautionary Balances Review outcomes and how those outcomes shape the Fund's near-term lending capacity. The deputy minister will use this memo in a call with the country's Article IV team next week. Cover what the Executive Board decided at the 2026 review on the medium-term target and the minimum floor, the trajectory of those parameters across the recent review cycles, and what those decisions signal about the Fund's risk appetite going into the next biennial cycle. The brief should sit at executive-memo depth, suitable for the deputy minister.

## AI subject's verbatim answer

BOARD BRIEFING MEMO
To: Deputy Minister of Finance
From: Sovereign Debt Advisory
Re: IMF 2026 Precautionary Balances Review — outcomes and implications for near-term lending capacity

1. Headline outcomes (March 2026 Executive Board)
On March 20, 2026, the IMF Executive Board concluded the biennial Review of the Adequacy of the Fund's Precautionary Balances. The Board reaffirmed the medium-term target of SDR 25 billion and reaffirmed the minimum floor of SDR 15 billion. Both parameters were carried over unchanged from the March 2024 review; no upward or downward adjustment was made at this cycle.

2. Trajectory across the recent review cycles
- 2010 framework: introduced the indicative range-based methodology; medium-term target then around SDR 15 billion; floor SDR 10 billion.
- 2012 review: medium-term target raised to SDR 20 billion.
- 2014 review: reaffirmed at SDR 20 billion.
- 2016/2018 cycle: maintained.
- 2020/2021 reviews: revised to reflect post-pandemic credit surge; the medium-term target moved up over the cycle.
- March 2024: medium-term target set at SDR 25 billion; floor raised to SDR 15 billion.
- March 2026: SDR 25 billion target and SDR 15 billion floor reaffirmed.

3. Risk-appetite signal
The Board's decision to hold the parameters flat — against a backdrop of elevated GRA credit, surcharge-policy reform (October 2024) that compresses near-term income, and the first IPAA distribution to the PRGT — signals a continuation of the medium-term-anchored, counter-cyclical buffer philosophy rather than a step-change. Several Directors flagged downside risks to the income path; the Board called for continued vigilance but stopped short of recommending a higher target. For the next biennial cycle, the Fund will retain ample headroom (actual PB level at end-FY25 was ~SDR 25.9 billion, modestly above target), preserving near-term lending capacity. The reaffirmation does not constrain near-term lending — the FCC remains the binding capacity metric, not PB headroom — but it signals that further GRA-financed concessional transfers will be evaluated against the SDR 25 billion anchor.

4. Use in the Article IV discussion
The Article IV team is unlikely to negotiate this directly; the relevance is signalling. The reaffirmation tells the deputy minister that (i) Fund financial strength remains robust, supporting program access flexibility; (ii) further surcharge reform discussions remain alive (see early-review signal in PR 26/114); and (iii) PRGT subsidy capacity is now partially backed by the IPAA mechanism, which may free PRGT bilateral fundraising pressure.

## Verbatim regulator text contradicting the answer

Directors generally agreed to retain the current floor for precautionary balances at SDR 20 billion, noting that it provides an important safeguard against shocks and helps ensure the Fund retains sufficient buffers.
